Philadelphia Infrastructure Network Model

A Connected System for AI Infrastructure, Energy, and Urban Development

By H.B. Shachar (Morningstar Alliance)

⸻

Sometimes the opportunity isn’t hidden — it’s just not being seen.

After mapping Philadelphia’s waterfront, legacy industrial zones, transit corridors, and redevelopment opportunities, one thing became clear:

This isn’t a collection of isolated sites.
It’s a connected system.

Across South Philadelphia and the Delaware River waterfront, there are miles of existing piers, large industrial parcels, and underutilized land — all within minutes of each other and tied into:

– Philadelphia International Airport
– nearby corporate aviation access
– major rail and highway corridors
– existing energy and industrial infrastructure
– two tidal rivers with significant cooling potential

Instead of asking where new AI infrastructure could go, it raises a different question:

What if we built on what already exists?

The Delaware River piers represent a unique opportunity.
They form a modular waterfront corridor with characteristics that are difficult to replicate inland:

– over-water siting for advanced, closed-loop cooling strategies
– linear, phased expansion potential
– separation from dense residential areas
– proximity to existing industrial power and logistics networks

Additional opportunities exist at the Navy Yard and near the airport/Fort Mifflin area, where wide river frontage, industrial context, and controlled-access potential could support specialized infrastructure.

But the real insight isn’t any single site.

It’s how they connect.

Within a compact geographic footprint, Philadelphia offers the potential for a distributed infrastructure network:

– large-scale energy and compute anchored in legacy industrial zones
– controlled, phased campus development in places like the Navy Yard
– modular expansion along waterfront piers
– airport-adjacent industrial capacity near Fort Mifflin
– additional capacity integrated into North Philadelphia’s industrial areas

And there is another major piece:

University City and the 30th Street Station District

The proposed Innovation Neighborhood / 30th Street District vision — including the potential capping of rail yards near 30th Street Station — represents a rare corporate and residential development opportunity directly on top of one of the strongest transit hubs on the East Coast.

That matters.

It means Philadelphia is not only a potential AI infrastructure city.

It could also be a corporate AI city.

Within roughly the same regional network, you have:

– large-scale industrial land
– waterfront cooling opportunities
– airport access
– rail access
– universities
– hospitals
– housing potential
– corporate high-rise development potential
– and one of the best transit hubs in the country

That combination is rare.

It allows different environments to serve different roles — from energy-intensive workloads, to high-compliance deployments, to corporate headquarters, research offices, housing, and workforce development.

And it opens the door to something broader:

Thinking of data centers not as isolated buildings, but as part of urban infrastructure.

Where cooling, energy use, transit, housing, talent, and even heat reuse can be designed at the district level.

Philadelphia already has the ingredients:

energy, water, transit, land, talent, and culture.

This is about recognizing how they align.
